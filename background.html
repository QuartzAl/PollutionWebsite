Surabaya

Overview


The background of our country is very populated, meaning it has a lot of people that are in our country, a lot of people means a lot of people to feed, for them to use products, and after usage of these products they can choose to throw it away or either throw it in the right spot. But from the usual observation from looking around the city, people don’t really care about the cleanliness of their environment, thus polluting the area. People in Indonesia, especially in Surabaya, already produce a lot of trash every month. This trash will then cover the grates in our roads, making the water unable to drain. This will create a flood that persists for hours in our city. Without any action and the same behavior throughout the years, nothing will change and our streets will continue to be filled with water and trash. Not only that, is that we know factories, fast food restaurants, malls, transportation vehicles, airports, opening new areas for work employment, and etc. These all produce some sort of carbon emissions, carbon emission can also cause pollution, but it’s a different type of pollution from before, not land, but air pollution. Air pollution will change the temperature of the air, a change in temperature will alter the formation of clouds, making it rain less or rain more. This could then also lead to inconsistent harvest times for the field of agriculture. Inconsistent agricultural practices could lead to them taking extra counter measures, thus, creating more carbon emissions for the extra resources they use. This is what causes Surabaya to be very polluted, other cities that aren’t as big don’t have all these access to machines and factories, and they are almost pollution free. Floods are all managed by the plants and their unclogged drains. Pollution of the air could also cause tornadoes to happen more frequently because air pollution doesn’t let cold air to gather underneath storm clouds. So cold air will be free to move in according to the hot air and thus creating more tornadoes. Indonesia however, has mostly hot air and rarely gets tornadoes either way. Landslides are also a given, since waste, deforestation, erosion can disrupt the integrity of the soil and make the earth less stable. Acid rain is caused when the polluted water evaporates to the clouds above to be rained upon us again. Acid rain can cause erosion and other environmental damage because of the dangerous chemicals it pours down.


Impact


Drowning accounts for 75% of deaths in flood disasters. Flood disasters are becoming more frequent and this trend is expected to continue. Drowning risks increase with floods particularly in low- and middle-income countries where people live in flood prone areas and the ability to warn, evacuate, or protect communities from floods is weak or only just developing.


Deaths also result from physical trauma, heart attacks, electrocution, carbon monoxide poisoning or fire associated with flooding. Often, only immediate traumatic deaths from flooding are recorded.


Floods can also have medium- and long-term health impacts, including:


  • water- and vector-borne diseases, such as cholera, typhoid or malaria
  • injuries, such as lacerations or punctures from evacuations and disaster cleanup
  • Chemical Hazards
  • mental health effects associated with emergency situations
  • disrupted health systems, facilities and services, leaving communities without access to health care
  • damaged basic infrastructure, such as food and water supplies, and safe shelter

Response


The magnitude of the physical and human costs from floods can be reduced if adequate emergency prevention, preparedness, response and recovery measures are implemented in a sustainable and timely manner.


WHO works with Member States to build resilient and proactive health systems that can anticipate the needs and challenges during emergencies so that they are more likely to reduce risks and respond effectively when needed.


Actions to be taken includes but are not limited to:


  • ensure appropriate food supplementation
  • restore primary care services, like immunization, child and maternal health, and mental health
  • assemble mobile health teams and outreach
  • conduct epidemic surveillance, early warning and response
